INTRODUCTION

Today I want to tell you how to downsample and bit depth of music files in such a way that it does not lead to serious loss of quality. This was already mentioned in the articles Downsizing systems in mastering and Sampling rate converters (from the magazine "Sound engineer"), where you can also find detailed information on the principles of the algorithms described here.

Mainly I would like to clarify and show how the problem can be solved by more or less "improvised" means, namely, using only foobar2000 (this possibility once again confirms the enormous potential of this player).

So, let's say you have music material in Hi-Res ("high-resolution audio", "high-resolution sound" - Sony suggested that this is the name of all formats with a bit depth / sampling rate of at least 24 bit / 96 kHz) and you need play it on a device that does not support the available bit depth / sampling rate / number of channels, or burn it onto an audio CD. If you just load it into a sound editor and then save it in 16 bit / 44.1 kHz format, it will introduce quite a lot of distortion. Don't believe me? Let me give you an example.

The original tone spectrum with a frequency of 1 kHz with a level of -90 dBFS in 24/96 format (it should be noted that this sound is easy to hear on sound equipment of medium and high quality, provided there is a low noise level):

As you can see in the graph, the spurious harmonics are about 60 dB below the desired signal level and should not be heard even when the audio volume is increased.

And here is the spectrum obtained by loading / saving in 16 bit / 44.1 kHz with the Sony Sound Forge 10 program:

I must say that the same spectrum can be obtained by incorrect conversion by the foobar2000 program, but more on that later. As you can see, the level of distortion (relative to the useful signal) has increased significantly. They are mainly due to the lowering of the bit depth by simply discarding the lower eight bits, as well as resampling (to a lesser extent, the used resampling algorithm is of sufficient quality). If you listen to this signal, it will be far from pure tone by ear. What to do? How to lower the bit depth (in this case, the bit depth is the main factor) and not lose the sound quality of quiet sections?

This is where dithering and noise shaping technologies come to the rescue , which have already been mentioned more than once in the articles on this site. Above, we considered the option of saving audio in the desired format using the Sony Sound Forge program, this in no way proves that the program "does not know how" to correctly lower the bit rate, I just would like to describe an easier way than using the special functions of Sound Forge. more, this editor is paid).

So, these technologies, and even in combination with a high-quality resampler and channel mixer, are also supported by foobar2000.

Converter settings

Necessary software and plugins

foobar2000 (set in Full or Normal mode)

Third party plugins:

SoX Resampler (foo_dsp_resampler.dll) - downsampling;
Matrix Mixer (foo_dsp_mm.dll) - converting the number of channels;
DVD-Audio Decoder (foo_input_dvda.dll) - decoding DVD Audio;
HDCD decoder (foo_hdcd.dll) , DTS Decoder (foo_hdcd.dll) , SACD Decoder (foo_input_sacd.dll) , etc. - decoder / preprocessor for decoding the source - HDCD , DTS SACD or others, including PCM representation.

You can download all this on the foobar2000 + plugins page .

The main thing is to remember the principle: for the front channels and the subwoofer, the coefficient is 1 , for the rear center - 0.5, for all others - 0.707 . You can read more about surround sound on Wikipedia .

Also pay attention to the Normalize checkbox - this option automatically underestimates the values ​​of the coefficients so that their total value for the existing channels gives one. This avoids exceeding the 0 dBFS level and eliminates the need to fight clipping with level limiters, but can lead to relatively low volume levels. Conclusion

After creating a preset, everything is very simple. You need to download the files intended for converting to foobar2000, select them, right-click on the selection and select our profile:

And what do we get as a result? Here is the spectrum of the resulting file (the source is the test file mentioned at the beginning of the article):

The sound of such a signal will be practically indistinguishable from the original. Of course, at the cost of a little noise, but located in the frequency range to which the human ear is least sensitive.

As a result, quiet sounds will sound quite natural, just like in 24-bit audio, and noise (due to its shaping) will be beyond the human hearing threshold.

Finally, I would like to say that the 16-bit / 44.1 kHz standard is quite sufficient to get good sound, but subject to proper mastering (with high bit depth and sampling rate) and conversion to the final form using high-quality resampling algorithms, as well as Dithering and Noise Shaping technologies. ...
